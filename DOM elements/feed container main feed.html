    Researchers at UT Health San Antonio recently published findings on chronic stress and cognition:

    When your nervous system stays in constant stress mode, it contributes significantly to cognitive impairments across multiple disorders.

    Most high-performers don't realize this connection.
    You feel exhausted but can't sleep.
    Every task feels urgent.
    Every notification feels like a threat.
    Every morning feels heavier than it should.

    This isn't poor sleep hygiene.
    This is nervous system dysregulation.

    And here's what matters:
    You cannot "optimize" your way out of a dysregulated nervous system.
    You can't force better sleep. You can't bio-hack away chronic stress activation.

    That's like trying to sleep peacefully while your body thinks you're being chased by a predator.

    Why this happens:
    Chronic stress activates your sympathetic nervous system and HPA axis.
    Your body stays in fight-or-flight mode.
    Your brain switches from rest → protect mode.
    Cortisol stays elevated when it should drop.
    You feel wired, exhausted, and can't shut down.

    What actually works:
    4-7-8 breathing before bed (4-second inhale, 7-second hold, 8-second exhale)
    Consistent sleep anchor every night, weekends included
    60-90 minute wind-down buffer with no screens

    Real-time stress resets during the day to prevent evening buildup
    You don't need another sleep tracker.
    You need your system to downshift.

    Europe doesn't have a startup problem.
    It has a mindset problem.

    I often get asked if I miss New York.
    What I miss MOST is what it does to your thinking.

    In NYC, ideas expand.
    In Europe, they get audited.

    The differences are subtle, but they compound:
    Every conversation in NYC builds on your idea.
    In Europe, the first response is often a risk list.

    Not transactional networking, but people sharing openly, about real things, and what they can do for others.

    That’s where mindset shifts start.

    8h •

    Most supplements marketed for brain health have little to no rigorous evidence behind them, except for a few. Creatine is one of the exceptions worth an honest conversation. People know creatine as something athletes take to build muscle. What most people don't realize is that the brain runs on the same energy system that creatine supports. Think back to your high school biology. ATP, adenosine triphosphate, is the molecule your cells use to produce energy. Your brain is the most metabolically demanding organ in your body. It runs on a constant supply of ATP. And creatine plays a direct role in replenishing that supply, particularly under conditions of cognitive stress and high demand. Two large meta-analyses now support this. A systematic review published in Nutrition Reviews analyzed 10 randomized controlled trials in healthy individuals and found that creatine supplementation significantly improved memory performance. The overall effect was meaningful. But what stood out was the subgroup finding. In older adults aged 66-76, the effect size was nearly three times that in younger adults. That is not a trivial finding. That is exactly the age range where cognitive protection matters most. A second meta-analysis published in Frontiers in Nutrition examined 16 randomized controlled trials involving 492 adults across a broader range of cognitive outcomes. It confirmed significant improvements in memory and information processing speed. The effects were most pronounced among people with underlying health conditions and adults aged 18 to 60. Taken together, the data is consistent. That said, I don’t recommend creatine broadly or casually. The effect size for overall cognitive function and executive function remains modest. It is not a dementia treatment. It should not be framed as one. Larger trials across more diverse patient populations are still needed. For patients who want to take creatine, I always stress these things: ↳Sleep, nutrition, metabolic health, exercise, and stress management come first. No supplement replaces that foundation. What follows is relevant only once those basics are genuinely in place. ↳Hydration is non-negotiable. Creatine increases water retention and places additional demand on the kidneys if fluid intake is inadequate. ↳Special consideration is needed for patients with pre-existing kidney disease. This is not a supplement to trial casually in that population. ↳GI side effects like bloating, nausea, and loose stools are common early on and should be monitored. The bar for recommending a supplement in my practice is high, and creatine clears it. It’s definitely not a magic pill but a tool with authentic data behind it, for the right person with the basics in place.
